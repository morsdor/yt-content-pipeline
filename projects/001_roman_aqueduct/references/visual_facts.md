## The Pont du Gard (hero structure)
- ✅ **Exactly THREE tiers of arches** — never two, never four.
- ✅ Arch counts per tier: **bottom 6 · middle 11 · top originally ~35** (many small top arches now lost —
  show it intact/whole for the "as built" hero, weathered for a "today" shot; pick per plate).
- ✅ **48.8 m tall** — it is TALL relative to its length; do not draw it long-and-low.
- ✅ The **water channel (*specus*) runs along the very TOP tier**, capped by stone slabs — not through the
  middle, not below. The water is *up top*, out of sight.
- ✅ **Dry-stone ashlar in the big arches — no mortar** in the large spans; huge precisely-cut blocks
  (some ~6 tonnes). Reads as clean, tight stone joints, not rubble.
- ✅ **Protruding stone bosses / putlog stubs** stick out from the piers (scaffolding supports left in place)
  — a signature detail; include them for authenticity.
- ✅ **Warm honey / golden shelly limestone** (local Estel quarry stone) — this IS the "warm ochre/terracotta"
  brand accent. Water = the electric-blue pop.
- ✅ **Arch spans are slightly UNEQUAL** — the arches are not a perfectly uniform arcade; the largest span
  straddles the river. Subtle, but avoid a mechanically identical repeat.
- ✅ The **river Gardon flows underneath**, through the gorge the bridge crosses.

## The channel (*specus*) — for cutaway/interior plates
- ✅ **Roughly 1.2–1.8 m wide, ~1.8 m tall** — a person can walk/stoop inside. Human-scale, not a pipe.
- ✅ Lined with **opus signinum** — pinkish/red waterproof hydraulic mortar (crushed-tile aggregate).
- ✅ **Thick calcium-carbonate sinter deposits** on the walls (layered, cream/tan) — centuries of lime scale;
  a great "this really carried water for 500 years" detail on a cutaway.
- ✅ Covered top (slabs / vault) — it is an enclosed conduit, not an open trough, along most of the run.

## Surveying instruments (for the "how they held the slope" beat)
- ✅ **Chorobates** — a long (~6 m / 20 ft) wooden **bench/table on legs**, with a **water-filled groove
  along the top** and **plumb-lines hanging at each end** against marked notches. The star instrument.
- ✅ **Groma** — a vertical staff with a **horizontal cross** on top, **four plumb-bobs** hanging from the
  arms, mounted on an **offset bracket** (so the surveyor sights past the pole). For straight lines / right angles.
- ✅ **Dioptra** — a sighting instrument (sighting tube / alidade) on a stand for measuring angles. More
  complex/geared than the other two; use sparingly, it's the least iconic.

## Landscape / composition truths
- ✅ The aqueduct **meanders along contours** — for any "whole route" map/wide shot, it is a *winding* line
  hugging hills, NOT a straight shot from Uzès to Nîmes (straight-line ~20 km vs ~50 km built).
- ✅ Terrain = **limestone garrigue** — scrubby, dry, rocky Mediterranean hill country (olive/scrub, pale rock).
- ⚠ Do **not** draw a Rome-style multi-arch arcade marching across open plains for the *whole* aqueduct —
  most of it is **underground / at grade**; the tall arcade is the *exception* (river crossings like the
  Pont du Gard), not the rule. Getting this right is the "what most people miss" visual.
