## Research notes — the four pillars (brand_guide §7 beat sheet)

### 1. The constraint — *why it was hard*
Deliver water to Nemausus (Nîmes) across ~50 km of irregular limestone *garrigue* — with only **~12–17 m of
total height to spend** (⚠ figure contested, see Numbers). The straight-line distance from the source
(Fontaine d'Eure, Uzès) to Nîmes is only ~20 km; the channel runs **~50 km because it must hug the land
contours** to bleed off height slowly enough. The tolerance is brutal on both sides: too steep and the water
runs fast, scours the mortar lining and undercuts the channel; too shallow and it silts, stagnates, and stops.
They had to hold an average slope finer than a modern highway's drainage tolerance — **by hand, with a trough
of water and plumb-lines, across hills they could not see over.**

### 2. The mechanism — *how it worked*
**Pure gravity, no pumps, end to end.** One continuous downhill ramp from spring to city.
- **Survey:** stake a downhill contour line with three instruments — **chorobates** (a ~6 m / 20-foot bench
  with a water-filled groove = a long spirit level; Vitruvius called it the most reliable tool), **groma**
  (a cross with four plumb-bobs for straight lines and right angles), **dioptra** (a sighting device for
  angles). The *libratores* (levellers) leap-frogged these down the whole route to hold the grade.
- **Carry:** a masonry channel (*specus*), ~1.2–1.8 m wide and tall enough to walk inside (~1.8 m), lined with
  waterproof **opus signinum** (hydraulic mortar with crushed tile). Mostly underground or at grade.
- **Cross:** where the **Gardon river gorge** blocks the line, they did **not** drop down and siphon back up
  (they knew how — see below). They carried the channel *across at grade* on a bridge — **the Pont du Gard**:
  three tiers of arches, **48.8 m high**, the *specus* running along the very top under stone cap-slabs.

### 4. The human angle — *the witness* (brand_guide §7 cold-open)
Two options, pick at script:
- **The libratore at dawn.** An anonymous Roman *levelling-man* hunched over the chorobates trough at first
  light — because still, cold air means dead-flat water and no shimmer. He sights a line downhill he can't
  see the end of, and the whole city's water rides on his eye. (Best "insane precision" cold-open — puts a
  human at the instrument.)
- **Frontinus.** Sextus Julius Frontinus, Rome's *curator aquarum* (~97 AD), who wrote *De aquaeductu* — a
  real, named Roman obsessed with water accounting, fraud, and to-the-digit precision. Great voice for the
  "precision as bureaucratic obsession" angle, though he's Rome, not Nîmes. (Use as narrator-voice, not the
  cold-open witness.)

## What most people miss — the engineer's-eye take
**The arches are the least impressive part.** Everyone photographs the Pont du Gard's arches — but the
bridge is just the **456 m** where the slope had to cross a river. It's a rounding error on a 50 km line.
The real feat is **invisible**: a team with a trough of water surveyed a downhill path so precise that across
that entire, world-famous bridge, the water falls **the thickness of two stacked coins**. And they did it on
purpose the *hard* way — they knew how to build a pressurised siphon (Lyon's aqueducts used several), which
would have let them take a shorter, steeper line. They chose the long, gentle, hand-held contour and a bridge
instead — arguably because an open channel is far easier to clean and maintain than a buried lead siphon.
**The genius is a decision and a gradient, not a row of arches.**

*(Supporting fact for the "they knew how to siphon" beat: inverted siphons were real Roman/Hellenistic tech —
the Aspendos aqueduct had a 1,670 m stone siphon with two ~40 m pressure towers; the Hellenistic siphon at
Pergamon reached ~19 bar at ~190 m head. Nîmes itself used **none** — pure gravity + the bridge.)*

   siphon**. (Siphons existed elsewhere — good contrast beat, not part of this line.)
