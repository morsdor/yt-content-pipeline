## 1. One-line snapshot

Chand Baori is a square, inverted-pyramid **stepwell** in the village of **Abhaneri, Rajasthan** — ~**3,500 steps** in a precise geometric lattice descending **~13 storeys** to a water tank at the bottom. Built roughly **8th–9th century CE**. Its job: reach a **water table that rises and falls with the seasons** in an arid land, by turning a fixed well into an adaptive staircase.

## 3. Build date / era  ✅ mostly solid, one honest caveat

- **8th–9th century CE.** Wikipedia's infobox gives *construction started c. 800, completed c. 900.*
- Named after **Raja Chanda** (a.k.a. Chand) of the **Nikumbha (Nikumbh) Rajput dynasty** — "Chand" = the ruler, "baori/baoli" = stepwell.
- ⚠️ **Caveat worth keeping honest on-screen:** *No epigraphic (inscription) evidence exists* for the construction of either the baori or the adjoining Harshat Mata temple. The 8th–9th-century date is inferred **by stylistic comparison** with the terraced temples of **Paranagar and Mandore**. → The blog-common precise year **"825 CE" is not firmly established** — recommend we *not* state a single year as fact.
- The stepwell was **likely built before** the adjoining Harshat Mata temple (Atherton).
- Livingston: Chand Baori has *"two classical periods of water building in a single setting"* — i.e. it was **built and extended over time**. An upper palace/pavilion with cusped ("tabulated") arches was added later under Chauhan-era rulers; the four-sided verandah is a later addition too. Good nuance: it's not a single-shot build.

## 4. Steps & stories  ✅ solid (the headline numbers)

- **~3,500 steps** — consistent across every source.
- **13 storeys / levels** deep. (Wikipedia: "3500 steps cascading 13 stories deep into a massive tank.")
- Structural detail: **double flights of steps on each of its ten landings**, on the three stepped sides. (So "13 storeys" is the popular depth count; "ten landings" is the built-terrace count — both are used. Recommend we say **"thirteen storeys"** as the vivid figure and can mention the terrace landings.)

## 5. Depth  ⚠️ REAL DISCREPANCY — please pick our number

- **~20 m (≈ 65 ft)** is the most consistent and most internally-plausible figure. One source gives **19.8 m** precisely.
- **BUT** Wikipedia's lead sentence says *"approximately 30 m (100 ft)."* Several blogs repeat "100 ft."
- **My engineer's-eye read:** 13 storeys × ~1.5 m per terrace ≈ **19–20 m**, which matches the 65-ft figure. The 30 m / 100 ft number implies ~2.3 m per storey, which is too tall for these terraces. **Recommend we say "about twenty metres — roughly thirteen storeys down," and avoid the 100-ft claim** (or explicitly note it as a common overstatement). Your call — this is the single number most likely to be wrong in the final cut, so flagging it hard.

## 6. Plan dimensions & geometry  ✅ solid

- **Square plan, ~35 m on each side** (widely cited; medium-high confidence — repeated across tourism/engineering sources, not in the Wikipedia body I pulled).
- **Three sides** (east, west, south) carry the **double flights of steps** in a tessellated, zig-zag **"diamond-setting"** pattern — the mesmerising visual. Vastu texts (*Aparajitaprccha* / Vishvakarma's *Vastushastra*) name this design a **"vijay vapi."**
- **Fourth side (north):** a **multi-storey pillared corridor / gallery**, plus a **haveli pavilion and resting room** for royalty. Two niche-shrines (Ganesha; Mahishasuramardini/Durga), and a **reclining Vishnu (Sheshasayee, on the serpent Ananta)** image. Later beautification/repairs continued into the late medieval period.
- Overall form: an **inverted / upside-down pyramid** — widest at the top, narrowing down to the tank. The geometry has been called *"a mathematical marvel"* (Rana Safvi) — good hook territory.

## 7. The water-table engineering  ✅ THE HEART OF THE VIDEO

- **The constraint:** Rajasthan is arid. Dependable fresh water is essentially seasonal — it arrives with the **monsoon** and then the **water table falls, sometimes far,** through the long dry months. Surface rivers/creeks dry up entirely.
- **Why an ordinary vertical well is a poor answer:** a normal well gives you *one fixed draw point*. When the water table drops metres below it, a fixed platform strands you above the water.
- **The stepwell's move:** instead of one platform, build a **giant descending staircase** so that **whatever level the water sits at, in any season, there is always a step at the waterline.** High water after the monsoon → you stop near the top. Low water in high summer → you keep walking down. **The steps *are* the interface with a moving water surface.** (This is the "what most people miss" candidate — see §9.)
- **How it's built to work:** trenches dug deep to reach **year-round groundwater**; walls lined with **stone blocks laid WITHOUT mortar.** Because it's mortarless, **groundwater below the water-table line filters through the stone into the tank** — the structure is permeable by design. (Bonus: mortarless masonry also flexes rather than shattering — stepwells are noted for surviving earthquakes.)
- **Thermal payoff:** at the bottom the air stays **5–6 °C cooler** than the surface; the well doubled as a **cool community gathering place** in fierce heat, and (sunk + shaded) it **loses far less water to evaporation** than an open tank would.
- **Social/religious role:** water-drawing (mostly by **women**), rest stop for **pilgrims/travellers**, and ritual life tied to the **Harshat Mata** ("goddess of joy") temple next door.

## 9. Candidate "WHAT MOST PEOPLE MISS" (your engineer's-eye take — you'll rewrite this)

Recommended framing (pick/rewrite in your voice):

> **The staircase isn't the way to the water — it *is* the water infrastructure.** A vertical well solves a fixed-depth problem. Chand Baori solves a *moving-boundary* problem: a water table that swings tens of feet between monsoon and peak summer. It does it with **geometry and zero energy** — a passive machine that automatically presents a fresh waterline at whatever height the water happens to be. Tourists see a hypnotic pattern or a punishing climb; an engineer sees an **adaptive interface engineered around a variable.**

Alternative angles if you'd rather:
- **Mortarless-on-purpose:** the "unfinished-looking" dry masonry is the feature — it lets groundwater seep in *and* lets the structure survive earthquakes. Weakness reframed as design.
- **Evaporation, not just access:** in a desert the real enemy is the sun. Sinking the reservoir 20 m down and shading it is as much about *keeping* water as *reaching* it.
- **Fractal edge:** the zig-zag double flights massively increase the *length of waterline* available at any given level — many people can draw at once. The pattern is throughput, not decoration.
